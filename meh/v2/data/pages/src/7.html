Short description.

This tool is a tool used for typing the symbols of the MELFA virtual dialect, based on the J.R.R. Tolkien's Tengwar scripting system (alphabet).

Full description.

MELFA is a virtual dialect which uses the J.R.R. Tolkien's Tengwar scripting system (alphabet). MELFA dialect uses the full writing mode, that means, all the vowel sounds are written as separate letters, not as the diacritic signs (tehtar). MELFA uses a modified version and a mixture of the Beleriand Mode and General Use Mode of the Tengwar. The basic vowel sound letters in MELFA are the same as in Mode of Beleriand, but the complicated vowels are different from the original Tolkien's scripts. The line under the vowel symbol means the mixture with the [i] sound at the beginning of the sound (ia, io, iu, ie), similar to (but not exact as) Finnish and German soft sounds with umlauts. Most of the consonants are taken from original Tolkien's Beleriand Mode of the Tengwar, but some consonants are different, among them is [z] which was taken from other Tengwar Mode. Short sounds like [j] and [w] are also not original ones. More information about the letters used in MELFA may be found in this file.

Aim.

The purpose of this writing system is a modification in order to use more vowel sounds than in the original J.R.R. Tolkien's works. It is known that J.R.R. loved Finnish language and considered it to be one of the most beautiful (for ears) languages in the world. Finnish language is rich of vowels and is very unique amongst the modern european languages. However, J.R.R. did not implement all the vowel sounds into his elvish fantasies. This scripting system tries to correct that mistake and tries to make elvish speak even more pleasant to human's ears.

Usage.

To use the tool you can try different methods. First of all, you may click on the images with letters and they will appear in the text. Secondly, you may click inside the text field and try to write in the "raw" font symbols, but I do not recommend this and will tell the reason later. Thirdly, you can click mouse button somewehere on the page outside the text area (to make it loose the focus), outside the buttons with letters, and then press buttons on the keyboard. The letters will appear inside the text field.

And now, the explainations. Why not use the second method? The symbols of the font are binded to various characters which are not there, where we may think they are. And about the third method. Third method is working only for those letters that are on keyboard. Symbols that have no latin equivalents can not be written this way. So, to sum up, the best way to enscribe letters is to use a hybrid technique: those simple letters which have latin equivalents may be typed using the third method. Other letters may be put by mouse clicks on images.
